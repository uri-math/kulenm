Some Applications of Game Theory



 
 

"Drama Theory": This theory is an extension of traditional Game Theory, but which seeks to take into account the emotions and past interactions of the players involved.

Traditional Game Theory treats each player as a rational being and works out the best possible strategy for that player to fulfill his/her goals (i.e. maximize their payoff). This, however, is rarely the case in real-life human interaction, where tempers can flare, grudges are held, and players will make what seem to be irrational decisions. For instance, in the case of Jack and Jill discussed in our examples, Jack may have an ulterior motive to let Jill win more times than she loses. Perhaps the way to Jill's heart is through her purse strings and Jack is smitten. In this case, Jack's actions may not at first sight appear rational (i.e. his payoffs are going to be reduced), but there may be other payoffs.

In the case of the prisoner's dilemma, one "partner" in the crime spree might be decidedly angry at the other and always choose to rat him/her out. While this strategy (confessing) meshes with the best pure strategy we had decided on before, it was reached by different means and ultimately has a different meaning.

All this talk of emotional baggage may seem rather touchy-feely and far from the apparently solid mathematical results of traditional Game Theory. Recent research, however, has been able to show how tying emotions and an "irrational" preference for new payoffs results in one game simply being transformed into a new game with a new payoff matrix. Are we, then, on the verge of a theory of all interaction? Well...

The soundest criticism of Drama Theory comes in its desire to include all variables but its clear inability to do so. It would be surprising if, in any situation, all of the emotions and motives of the players were revealed. By taking some into account, however, we can more closely approximate reality. And that's the point, isn't it?
 

"Biological Evolutionary Game Theory":As outlined and expressed by biologist John Maynard Smith, Game Theory can be applied to a myriad of biological systems whereby organisms are competing for resources, mates, and survival (the stuff of evolution).

The main difference between traditional Game Theory and its application to biology is that it is no longer possible to impose rational thought on the players involved. Rather, Maynard Smith refers to the optimal strategy as an "Evolutionarily Stable Strategy" (ESS).

One example of such a strategy can be seen in the almost sweeping biological rule that offspring are split evenly between male and female. That is, there is a 0.5 chance of any one infant (and ultimately adult) being of a particular sex. Why should this be so? Maynard Smith asks us to consider the alternative: "Suppose that the population consisted mostly of females; then an individual which produced only sons would have more grandchildren. In contrast, if the population consisted mostly of males, it would pay to have daughters. If, however, the population consisted of equal numbers of males and females, sons and daughters would be equally valuable. Thus a 1:1 sex ratio is the only stable ratio; it is an "evolutionarily stable strategy."

Game Theory does more, though. It can be used to examine a myriad of interactions between individual organisms (sexual competition between males, seemingly altruistic behavior) as well as larger pictures of entire ecosystems. It can explain mathematically why two competing species will tend to become specialists on different resources, even though in isolation each species would be a generalist. In this regard, Game Theory takes into account what others are doing to determine the ESS. (As Maynard Smith says: "If everyone else is eating spinach it will pay to concentrate on cabbage.")

"Game Theory in Economics":As evidenced by John C. Harsanyi, John F. Nash, and Reinhard Selten winning the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1994 for their work in Game Theory, this mathematical framework serves well those interested in rigorously examining strategies in business and the economy.

Some of Game Theory's applications include assessing the various strategies in negotiations - who has the power and what is the best option for all parties involved? As shown in our simplified Weeble Wobble example, Game Theory can be used to identify the best strategy for businesses in competition with one another as well as point out the occasional instance where cooperation would benefit both or all players.
That Game Theory can shed tremendous light on Economics is well documented and a number of books are available for further research.

"Political Game Theory": In the political arena, decisions are made every day, which weigh the good of some over the good of some others. In choosing between two evils, which strategy results in the largest payoff (or lowest cost)? Game Theory can help.

First, Game Theory can help candidates decide how to campaign. How should campaigns allocate their resources? A recent study showed that a disproportionate amount of money was being spent in large states and a better strategy (with the maximum payoff being successful election) was developed.

Additionally, decision making in majority rule situations may at first sight appear cumbersomely large, each voter being regarded as a player. These situations can be reduced to a two-player zero-sum game with little difficulty, however, when one considers that voters more than often stay within their party lines. We end up with a "donkey vs. elephant" game which can be examined in the usual way.
