MORSE'S THEOREM
 

Morse Functions are stable and dense in the set of all smooth funcions. Furthermore, near a critical point of indexk there is a smooth change of coordinates underwhich the Taylor series of the Morse function f near the origin is the pure quadratic form
 

f (x 1 , x 2 , ... , x n ) = - x 12  - x 22  - ... - - x k2 +  x k+12 +  x k+12 + ... + x n2 .

Under this coordinate change the tayl of f vanishes.
 

Morse functions are functions that have only non-degenerate critical points. Degenerate critical points can be transformed into non-degenerate points by some small distortion. One must find the simplest way of transforming the point into a non-degenerate point.

Morse theory allows us to model reactions near critical points of real-world systems by using a simple quadratic. A mathematical formula for a seemingly complex system is at hand without searching through thousands of possible formulas to model the actions of the system near a particular point.
 
The question arises of how to handle smooth, non-Morse functions, or functions that have degenerate critical points. René Thom proposed the following theory:

 

Thom Classification Theorem

Up to multiplication by a constant and addition of a non-degenerate quadratic form, every

non-Morse function of codimension less than or equal to 4 is smoothly equivalent near

the origin to one of a set of standard forms.

 

These "standard forms" were classified by Thom and are given in the table below. Thom classified degerate, smooth functions by their degree of degeneracy denoted by corank/codimension. This enabled an equivelent non-degenerate function to be matched to the function at hand.
