APPLICATIONS AND EXAMPLES

 

 

Morse Theory has found its way into many areas of modern scientific analysis. The fields of topology and engineering make use of the ideas that Morse theory puts forth in order to describe and quantify systems or objects which occur naturally and may not have a concrete mathematical formula attributed to them. Some examples of these are fluid flow, biological processes, topology.

A readily available application of Morse Theory is in describing a surface of a folded or crumpled piece of paper. This in more technical terms is known as topology. When we crumple a piece of paper and examine it we see some reoccuring features. When describing the region near a specific point, we see that the paper lies flat near a point, the point lies on a fold in the paper, or a pleat is formed at the point. All other occurences can be transformed into one of these points. Therefore, a plane with random or naturally occuring features can be geometrically described by a finite number of equations describing the general features of the plane. The theory that all points on a smoothly transformed plane can be described as either regular points, folds, or cusps was put forth in 1955 by Hassler Whitney. He, however, based this theory in part on the findings of Marston Morse's work with critical points of smooth functions.

Mechanical engineering is constantly faced with the task of modeling an event or events occuring in nature in order to predict how a piece of machinery or structure will occur under various conditions especially near a critical point. For example, an automotive engineer designing an engine will need to know the dynamics of fluid flow between certain parts in order to design an effective lubrication system. Motor oil will flow between moving gears in a predictable pattern called stream lines. A point in which we want to study in this model would be stagnation points in flow. This is where upward flow is cancelled by downward flow to produce a point where there is effectively zero flow. We can model this flow by the use of stream lines which are given by a quadratic function. To learn about the stagnation point, we apply Morse's theory which states that a function will behave like a quadratic near a critical point. In this case the critical point is the stagnation point.
